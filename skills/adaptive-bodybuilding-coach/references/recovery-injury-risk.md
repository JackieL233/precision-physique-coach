# Recovery and Injury-Risk Management

## Recovery Inputs

Assess:

- Sleep duration and quality.
- Training volume and proximity to failure.
- Calorie deficit size.
- Daily steps/cardio load.
- Stress, alcohol, travel, illness, and schedule changes.
- Soreness, joint discomfort, motivation, and performance trend.

## Sleep

Use sleep as a primary recovery lever:

- Aim for a consistent schedule where possible.
- Reduce late caffeine if sleep quality is poor.
- In hard cutting or high-volume blocks, protect sleep before adding more training.

## Soreness

Delayed soreness is not the goal. Mild soreness can be normal after novel work, but persistent soreness that reduces performance means the dose may be too high.

Adjust by:

- Reducing sets for the sore muscle.
- Reducing range or load temporarily.
- Replacing the provocative exercise.
- Extending rest between sessions.

## Pain Rules

- 0-2/10: monitor if stable and not changing technique.
- 3-4/10: modify load, range, tempo, grip, stance, or exercise.
- 5+/10, sharp, worsening, swelling, instability, or altered mechanics: stop that movement.

Persistent or concerning pain belongs with a qualified clinician.

## Warm-Up

Use the smallest warm-up that improves performance and safety:

- General warm-up: 5-10 minutes easy movement if helpful.
- Specific warm-up: ramping sets for loaded exercises.
- Mobility drills only when they improve the exercise being trained.

## Fatigue Management

When recovery is poor, change the least disruptive variable:

- First: sleep, food timing, hydration, session length, exercise order.
- Then: reduce failure exposure and junk volume.
- Then: reduce weekly sets or cardio load.
- Finally: deload.

## Return After Break, Illness, or Injury

- Restart at 50-70 percent of previous volume.
- Keep several reps in reserve for the first week.
- Reintroduce high-skill and high-load movements gradually.
- Avoid "making up" missed workouts.

## Recovery Tools

Useful when they improve adherence or symptoms:

- Walking, easy cardio, light mobility, massage, heat/cold as preference.
- Do not let recovery tools replace appropriate programming, sleep, and nutrition.

## Injury-Risk Reduction Principles

- Progress load, volume, and novelty gradually.
- Keep movement quality stable under fatigue.
- Rotate exercises when joints dislike a pattern, but do not rotate so often that progress cannot be tracked.
- Match exercise choice to anatomy, equipment, and goal.
- Avoid repeated forced reps, max attempts, or failure on unstable compound lifts.
