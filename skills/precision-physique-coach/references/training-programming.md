# Training Programming

## Design Priorities

Build programs in this order:

1. Safety and adherence.
2. Goal-specific stimulus.
3. Recoverability.
4. Progressive overload.
5. Exercise skill and preference.
6. Efficient use of the user's equipment and time.

## Goal Modes

### Hypertrophy / Bodybuilding

- Use mostly 5-30 rep sets taken near failure.
- Most working sets should land around 1-3 RIR; use 0 RIR sparingly on stable isolation lifts.
- Start most trained lifters around 8-14 hard sets per muscle per week, then adjust from performance, soreness, pumps, joint tolerance, and measurements.
- Beginners often grow from 4-8 hard sets per muscle per week.
- Train each target muscle 2+ times per week when schedule allows.
- Use compounds for efficient loading and isolations for target volume with less systemic fatigue.

### Fat Loss With Muscle Retention

- Keep resistance training heavy enough to preserve performance.
- Reduce volume slightly if recovery drops; keep effort and technique high.
- Add steps/cardio progressively rather than immediately cutting calories aggressively.
- Prioritize protein, sleep, and consistent training.

### Recomposition

- Best for beginners, detrained users, higher body-fat users, or users returning after a break.
- Use maintenance or a small deficit/surplus depending on priority.
- Track strength and measurements, not scale weight alone.

### Strength-Biased Hypertrophy

- Include main lifts or close variants in lower rep ranges, then hypertrophy accessories.
- Avoid making every set maximal; use planned top sets, back-off work, and fatigue caps.

## Weekly Split Selection

- 2 days/week: full body twice.
- 3 days/week: full body or upper/lower/full.
- 4 days/week: upper/lower, push/pull/legs/upper, or torso/limbs.
- 5 days/week: upper/lower/push/pull/legs or body-part emphasis.
- 6 days/week: push/pull/legs repeat, upper/lower repeat, or specialization with careful fatigue management.

Match split to the user's calendar first. A perfect split the user cannot execute is a bad plan.

## Session Structure

1. Brief warm-up: 5-10 minutes easy cardio or movement prep.
2. Specific warm-up: ramp sets for first heavy movements.
3. Primary movement(s): high-skill compounds while fresh.
4. Secondary compounds.
5. Isolation and weak-point work.
6. Optional cooldown or mobility if useful.

## Progression Rules

Use double progression for most hypertrophy work:

- Give a rep range, such as 8-12.
- Keep load until all prescribed sets hit the top of the range at target RIR.
- Increase load modestly next exposure and allow reps to drop within range.

Use load progression for stable strength-biased main lifts:

- Increase load when bar speed, technique, and RIR target are maintained.
- If performance drops for two exposures despite good adherence, reduce load or volume.

Use volume progression only when needed:

- Add 1-2 sets per lagging muscle per week when recovery is good and progress is stalled.
- Remove sets when soreness lingers, joints complain, performance decays, or adherence suffers.

## Deload Criteria

Plan a deload after 4-8 hard weeks or trigger one when multiple signals appear:

- Performance drops across several sessions.
- Soreness lasts unusually long.
- Sleep, motivation, and appetite worsen.
- Joint discomfort rises.
- The user is in an aggressive cut or high life stress.

Deload by reducing volume 30-60 percent and keeping movement patterns. Intensity can be moderate; avoid grinding.

## Cardio and Steps

- For physique goals, prescribe cardio as a tool, not a punishment.
- Start with a sustainable step target and 2-4 cardio sessions if needed.
- Keep high-intensity intervals limited when leg recovery or lifting performance is a priority.

## Programming Checks

Every program should answer:

- Which muscles are prioritized?
- What weekly set target does each major muscle receive?
- How close to failure should sets be?
- How will the user progress?
- What data will decide the next adjustment?

## Evidence Anchors

- ACSM's 2026 resistance-training position stand emphasizes evidence-based resistance-training prescription for healthy adults across muscle function, hypertrophy, and physical performance.
- ACSM's earlier progression model remains useful for coaching language around progressive overload, exercise order, load, volume, rest, and frequency, but use current evidence and individual response when designing plans.
