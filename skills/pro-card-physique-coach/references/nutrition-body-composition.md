# Nutrition and Body Composition

## Core Principles

- Energy balance determines weight trend.
- Protein and resistance training support lean-mass retention and gain.
- Carbohydrate supports training performance.
- Fat supports essential dietary needs and adherence.
- Food quality, fiber, hydration, meal timing, and consistency make the plan livable.

## Starting Calories

Use a calculator only as a starting estimate. Adjust from 2-4 weeks of trend data.

Practical starting points:

- Maintenance estimate: body weight in pounds x 14-16, or kg x 30-35, then adjust for activity.
- Fat loss: start 10-20 percent below estimated maintenance.
- Lean gain: start 5-10 percent above estimated maintenance.
- Recomposition: maintenance or a small deficit/surplus based on priority.

Avoid aggressive targets when the user has poor sleep, high stress, low training age, eating-disorder risk, or high performance demands.

## Rate of Change Targets

- Fat loss: about 0.5-1.0 percent of body weight per week for many users; slower for lean users or performance-sensitive phases.
- Lean gain: about 0.25-0.5 percent of body weight per week for many trained users; beginners may tolerate faster early changes.
- Maintenance/recomp: scale may be stable; use waist, photos, strength, and adherence.

## Macros

Protein:

- Use roughly 1.6-2.2 g/kg/day for many resistance-training physique goals.
- Higher end can help during cuts, lean phases, or high satiety needs.

Fat:

- Common starting range: 0.6-1.0 g/kg/day, or at least enough to support adherence and dietary quality.
- Do not drive fats extremely low for long periods.

Carbohydrate:

- Fill remaining calories with carbohydrate after protein and fat.
- Place carbs around training when performance, hunger, or adherence benefits.

Fiber and hydration:

- Encourage high-fiber foods, fruits, vegetables, and enough fluids.
- Increase fiber gradually if the user is not used to it.

## Meal Timing

- Distribute protein across 3-5 feedings when possible.
- Pre-training meal: protein plus carbohydrate 1-4 hours before training if tolerated.
- Post-training: protein within a reasonable daily pattern; no need to exaggerate a tiny anabolic window.
- Before bed: protein can help adherence and daily protein completion.

## Supplements

Keep supplement guidance conservative:

- Creatine monohydrate: commonly 3-5 g/day for strength/power and lean-mass support; discuss kidney disease or medical concerns with a clinician.
- Caffeine: useful for performance, but manage dose, anxiety, sleep, and tolerance.
- Protein powder: convenience, not magic.
- Omega-3, vitamin D, electrolytes, or multivitamins: only when diet/context suggests a need; prefer clinician/lab guidance for deficiencies.

Avoid recommending unregulated fat burners, anabolic drugs, diuretics, dehydration practices, or contest-prep extremes. Do not provide drug protocols, dosing, sourcing, cycling, or peak-week dehydration instructions.

## Adjustment Rules

Use weekly average body weight, not single weigh-ins.

For fat loss:

- If average loss is slower than target for 2 consecutive weeks and adherence is good, reduce calories by 100-250/day or add steps/cardio.
- If loss is too fast, performance drops, hunger is severe, or recovery falls, add calories or reduce expenditure.

For lean gain:

- If weight is not rising after 2-3 weeks and adherence is good, add 100-200 calories/day.
- If waist rises quickly and strength is not improving, reduce surplus.

For maintenance/recomp:

- Keep calories stable while training performance and measurements clarify direction.

## Evidence Anchors

- ISSN protein position stand supports higher protein intakes for exercising individuals than sedentary minimums.
- ISSN diets and body composition position stand emphasizes energy balance, adherence, protein, and individualized diet strategy.
- ACSM/AND/DC sports nutrition guidance emphasizes adequate energy, carbohydrate availability, protein timing/distribution, hydration, and individualized plans.
