# Data Tracking and Adjustment

## Measurement Protocol

Track only data that will guide decisions.

Body weight:

- Weigh 3-7 mornings per week after bathroom, before food/drink.
- Use weekly average and trend, not single readings.

Measurements:

- Waist at a consistent landmark 1-2 times per week.
- Optional: hip, chest, arm, thigh, calf monthly or every 2-4 weeks.
- Use same tape tension and posture.

Photos:

- Same lighting, distance, poses, and time of day every 2-4 weeks.

Training:

- Exercise, load, reps, sets, RIR/RPE, rest, and notes on pain/technique.

Recovery:

- Sleep, stress, soreness, hunger, energy, libido/menstrual regularity where relevant, digestion, and adherence.

## Check-In Structure

Ask:

- What was the weekly average weight change?
- Did waist/measurements change in the expected direction?
- Did key lifts improve, hold, or regress?
- Was adherence at least 80-90 percent?
- How were hunger, energy, sleep, soreness, and pain?
- Did schedule or stress change?

## Decision Rules

Do not adjust from noisy single data points. Change one or two variables at a time.

Fat loss:

- On target: keep plan.
- Too slow for 2 weeks with good adherence: reduce 100-250 calories/day or add 1,500-3,000 steps/day or 1-2 cardio sessions.
- Too fast or performance/recovery poor: add 100-250 calories/day, reduce cardio, or reduce lifting volume slightly.

Lean gain:

- On target and strength improving: keep plan.
- No gain for 2-3 weeks with good adherence: add 100-200 calories/day.
- Waist rising too fast without performance gain: reduce 100-200 calories/day.

Training:

- Performance rising and recovery good: keep or gradually progress.
- Target muscle not progressing but recovery good: add a small amount of targeted volume.
- Performance falling and soreness high: reduce volume or deload.
- Joint pain: swap exercise and reduce provocative loading.

## Plateau Audit

Before changing the plan, check:

- Food logging accuracy and weekend drift.
- Scale consistency, sodium, menstrual cycle, constipation, creatine start, travel.
- Sleep and stress.
- Training effort and progression.
- Hidden cardio/step changes.

## Reporting Style

Be clear and coach-like:

- State the trend.
- State the likely cause.
- Make the smallest useful change.
- Define what data will prove the change worked.
