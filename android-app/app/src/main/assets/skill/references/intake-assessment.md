# Intake and Assessment

Use intake to make the plan personal, safe, and executable. Do not interrogate the user with every question at once unless they ask for a full form; collect the smallest dataset needed for the next decision.

## Minimum Intake

- Goal: hypertrophy, fat loss, recomposition, strength support, maintenance, return-to-training, contest/event prep, or unsure.
- Timeline: deadline, desired rate, and whether the deadline is flexible.
- Body data: age, sex, height, body weight, optional body-fat estimate, waist, photos availability.
- Training data: training age, current split, weekly sets per muscle, recent hard sets, key lifts, RIR/RPE familiarity, cardio/steps.
- Constraints: equipment, days/week, session length, schedule, travel, food access, budget, cooking ability.
- Recovery: sleep, stress, soreness, pain, work demands, alcohol, illness, menstrual cycle notes where relevant.
- Nutrition: typical intake, dietary pattern, restrictions, appetite, hunger, digestion, tracking willingness.
- Safety: red flags, medical conditions, medications, injuries, eating-disorder risk, pregnancy/postpartum status.

## Readiness Classification

Classify readiness before prescribing:

- **Green:** no red flags, stable schedule, consistent sleep, no current pain, clear equipment and goal.
- **Yellow:** minor pain, inconsistent schedule, high stress, poor sleep, uncertain intake, recent layoff, aggressive goal.
- **Red:** symptoms, acute injury, eating-disorder risk, extreme diet target, uncontrolled condition, or medical uncertainty.

Green users can start a normal plan. Yellow users need conservative volume, simpler nutrition, and short feedback loops. Red users need clinician clearance or a scope-limited educational response.

## Training Age

- Beginner: less than 6-12 months of consistent technique practice.
- Intermediate: can progress planned lifts, understands effort, has 1-4 years consistent training.
- Advanced: progress is slow, weak points are specific, fatigue management matters, often 4+ years consistent training.

Training age is behavioral, not just calendar time. A person who trained randomly for five years may still need beginner-level structure.

## Body-Composition Baselines

Use multiple baselines:

- Weekly average body weight.
- Waist measurement at a consistent landmark.
- Optional circumference sites: chest, hip, arm, thigh, calf.
- Photos with consistent lighting, posture, distance, and pose.
- Training performance in stable movements.
- Subjective markers: hunger, sleep, stress, soreness, confidence.

Avoid over-relying on body-fat scales or single photos.

## Constraint Mapping

Translate constraints into programming choices:

- Low time: fewer exercises, higher-quality sets, stable machines, paired non-competing movements.
- Home equipment: bodyweight, dumbbells, bands, unilateral work, tempo, pauses, high reps.
- Joint limitation: keep movement pattern goal, change implement, range, grip, stance, tempo, or loading profile.
- Low tracking willingness: use palm portions, plate method, protein anchors, and weekly weigh-ins.
- High tracking willingness: macros, food scale, detailed logs, trend analysis.

## Intake Output

After intake, summarize:

- Primary goal and tradeoffs.
- Safety status.
- Plan constraints.
- Starting assumptions.
- Missing data that would improve the next revision.
